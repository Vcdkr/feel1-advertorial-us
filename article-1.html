Let Me Back Up: I Was a Total Skeptic

I need to be completely honest with you here, because I want you to understand where I was coming from.

I am not a guy who spends money on skincare. I think most of the men's grooming industry is overpriced nonsense designed to prey on insecurity. I have a medicine cabinet with bar soap, a stick of deodorant, and a bottle of Gillette shaving gel I bought at Costco in 2023. That's it.

So when my wife, Lauren, started leaving articles on my iPad about "beef tallow skin care for men," I rolled my eyes so hard I nearly strained something.

Beef. Tallow. For my face.

I'm a grown man. I eat tallow. I don't put it on my face.

But Lauren is usually right about things, and I'd been noticing some changes I wasn't thrilled about. The lines around my eyes were deepening. The skin along my jaw was starting to look... loose. My forehead had a texture that wasn't there at 40. And every morning, I woke up looking dehydrated, even after eight hours of sleep.

I wasn't vain enough to care much, but I also wasn't going to lie to myself. My face was showing age faster than the rest of me.

Down the Rabbit Hole

One Saturday afternoon, I finally clicked one of the articles Lauren had bookmarked. What I found genuinely surprised me.

Beef tallow — the rendered fat from grass-fed cattle — has been used as a skin treatment for literally thousands of years. Ancient Romans used it. Indigenous cultures across every continent used animal fats to protect and nourish their skin. Before the petrochemical boom of the 1950s, tallow-based skincare was completely normal.

Then something interesting happened: the rise of synthetic cosmetics made natural fats unfashionable. Petroleum-based moisturizers were cheaper to manufacture and easier to market. Natural animal fats got replaced with mineral oil, synthetic emollients, and water-based creams that sit on top of your skin without actually penetrating it.

"Tallow's fatty acid profile is virtually identical to human sebum — the natural oil your skin produces. This gives it a bioavailability that synthetic creams simply cannot match."

— From a dermatology review I found in my reading

I'm not a scientist, but that made intuitive sense to me. If your skin naturally produces something similar to tallow, it would know what to do with it. Your skin recognizes it. It absorbs it. It uses it.

Synthetic creams, no matter how expensive, are essentially strangers knocking on the door. Tallow is family.
